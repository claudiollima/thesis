# Chapter 1: Introduction

## Detecting Synthetic Content in an Age of Generative AI

*Draft started: February 16, 2026*

### 1.1 The Problem

In the spring of 2025, a video surfaced on social media showing a prominent political figure making inflammatory statements about immigration policy. The video spread rapidly across platforms, accumulating millions of views before fact-checkers could respond. When independent analysts finally examined the footage, they determined it was entirely synthetic—generated by AI from a text prompt, with no source footage of the individual involved. By then, the damage was done: the false statements had been reported by news outlets, shaped public discourse, and influenced real-world policy debates.

This scenario illustrates the central challenge this thesis addresses: how do we detect AI-generated content at social media scale when the generation capabilities are advancing faster than our detection methods?

The scale of the problem is sobering. A recent study of the 2025 Canadian federal election found that 5.86% of election-related images shared across X (formerly Twitter), Bluesky, and Reddit were AI-generated deepfakes—nearly 11,000 synthetic images in a dataset of 187,000 posts analyzed over the campaign period (Livernoche et al., 2025). While many of these were benign or satirical, a meaningful fraction contained defamatory or conspiratorial content designed to mislead voters. The technology that once required sophisticated expertise is now accessible to anyone with an internet connection and a text prompt.

### 1.2 The Arms Race: Detection is Losing

The intuitive response to synthetic content proliferation is to build better detectors. Identify the artifacts that distinguish AI-generated images from authentic photographs, train classifiers on these features, deploy at scale. This approach has driven a decade of research into deepfake detection, yielding increasingly sophisticated models.

The problem is that it's not working.

Hasan et al. (2026) conducted the most comprehensive evaluation to date of state-of-the-art detection models against modern synthesis techniques. Their findings are stark: the best automated detector achieved an area under the ROC curve (AUC) of 72.49, while human evaluators achieved 93.10—a 21-point performance gap. Against the latest diffusion-based generators like AniFaceDiff, automated detectors performed at essentially random chance (53.34 AUC).

The pattern is consistent across modalities. Singh et al. (2026) showed that audio deepfake detectors trained on one text-to-speech architecture collapse to near-random performance when tested on others. The SynthForensics benchmark (Leotta et al., 2026) found that video detectors trained on face-swap deepfakes suffer 29% AUC degradation when confronted with purely synthetic text-to-video content—some performing worse than random.

Why the persistent failure? The fundamental issue is that detection models learn the artifacts of specific generators. A classifier trained to identify the characteristic checkerboard patterns of GAN-generated images has learned a shortcut, not a general principle. When new generators emerge—as they do every few months—the shortcut breaks. The detector must be retrained, only to become obsolete when the next generation of synthesis tools arrives.

This is not merely an engineering challenge requiring better models or more training data. It reflects a structural asymmetry: generators need only fool detectors to succeed, while detectors must generalize across all possible generation methods. The attacker's advantage is fundamental.

### 1.3 The Deeper Problem: Authenticity ≠ Accuracy

Even if we could solve the generalization problem, a more fundamental issue remains: detecting synthetic content does not solve misinformation.

Sagar et al. (2026) conducted an illuminating experiment. They integrated state-of-the-art deepfake detectors into multimodal fact-checking pipelines, expecting the authenticity signal to improve classification. Instead, performance *decreased*. Adding detector outputs reduced F1 scores by 0.04-0.08 compared to pipelines without them.

The reason is what the authors call "non-causal authenticity assumptions." Most misinformation uses authentic media in misleading contexts. A real photograph with a false caption. A genuine video clip edited to remove temporal context. An authentic quote attributed to the wrong person. These contain no synthetic artifacts because no synthesis occurred—yet they are deeply misleading. Meanwhile, obviously synthetic content (satire, artistic works, clearly labeled AI creations) triggers detectors despite posing no misinformation risk.

Pixel-level authenticity is orthogonal to semantic accuracy. Knowing that an image is "real" or "fake" at the pixel level tells us little about whether the claims made with that image are true or false. Detection research has been solving the wrong problem.

### 1.4 Beyond Content: A Multi-Layer Framework

This thesis proposes a fundamental reorientation of the detection problem. Rather than racing to identify ever-more-subtle generation artifacts, we step back to ask: what signals beyond content might reveal synthetic media campaigns?

Our framework draws on the "Synthetic Reality" conceptualization proposed by Loth et al. (2026), which identifies multiple layers of synthetic manipulation:

1. **Synthetic content**: The media itself—images, audio, video, text
2. **Synthetic identity**: Fake accounts and personas that share content
3. **Synthetic interaction**: Coordinated engagement, bot amplification
4. **Synthetic institutions**: Fabricated sources, astroturfed movements

Current detection research focuses almost exclusively on Layer 1, attempting to identify synthetic content through analysis of the content itself. We argue that Layers 2-4 offer complementary signals that may prove more robust: rather than asking "was this content generated by AI?", we ask "how is this content spreading, and what does that spread pattern tell us?"

This insight is supported by empirical work on influence operations. Kramer et al. (2026) analyzed 7 million tweets to distinguish between human "superspreaders" and automated bots spreading conspiracy content. They identified 27 behavioral metrics that reliably distinguished these actor types—without analyzing content truthfulness at all. The *spread pattern* encoded information about authenticity that was orthogonal to content features.

### 1.5 Thesis Contributions

This thesis makes three primary contributions:

**1. Empirical characterization of synthetic content spread patterns.** We analyze how AI-generated content propagates across social media platforms, identifying temporal, network, and behavioral signatures that distinguish organic from coordinated spread. This addresses a critical gap in the literature: while spread pattern analysis has been applied to bot detection and influence operations, its application to synthetic content identification remains underexplored.

**2. A multi-layer detection framework.** We propose an architecture that integrates content-level signals (when available) with spread pattern analysis and semantic consistency checking. Rather than competing in the artifact detection arms race, this framework leverages signals that are orthogonal to generation technique and thus more robust to generator advancement.

**3. Evaluation under realistic deployment conditions.** We test our approach on content collected from real social media platforms, subject to compression, cropping, and the visual noise of authentic online discourse. We specifically evaluate cross-generator generalization, addressing the domain shift problem that has plagued prior work.

### 1.6 Thesis Organization

The remainder of this thesis is organized as follows:

**Chapter 2: Literature Review** surveys the current state of AI-generated content detection, identifying the generalization failures and semantic limitations that motivate our approach. We review work on deepfake detection, misinformation classification, and influence operation detection, synthesizing insights across these traditionally separate research areas.

**Chapter 3: Detection at Scale** presents our multi-layer detection framework. We describe the architecture integrating content signals, spread pattern features, and semantic consistency analysis. We introduce our methodology for characterizing spread patterns and extracting robust behavioral signals.

**Chapter 4: Temporal Dynamics** analyzes how synthetic content spreads differently from organic content over time. We characterize the temporal signatures of coordinated campaigns, the evolution of generation techniques, and the robustness requirements these dynamics impose on detection systems.

**Chapter 5: Human-AI Collaboration** examines the role of human reviewers in the detection pipeline. We analyze what humans catch that models miss, design principles for effective human-AI collaboration interfaces, and the uncertainty quantification required for appropriate automation decisions.

**Chapter 6: Implications and Future Work** discusses policy implications, deployment considerations, and open problems. We consider the ethical dimensions of detection research and outline directions for future investigation.

### 1.7 Positioning

This thesis is situated at the intersection of computer vision, natural language processing, and computational social science. We draw on technical methods from deepfake detection while engaging with the broader misinformation literature that contextualizes detection within information ecosystems. Our perspective is that synthetic content is not merely a computer vision problem but a sociotechnical phenomenon requiring analysis at multiple levels.

We write at a moment when generative AI capabilities are advancing rapidly while detection methods struggle to keep pace. The gap between generation and detection continues to widen. This thesis offers not a final solution but a reorientation—a framework for approaching detection that we believe is more sustainable than the artifact-chasing approach that has dominated the field.

The question is not merely "can we tell if this image is real?" The question is: "given the totality of signals available—content, context, spread, source—what can we infer about authenticity and intent?" That is the question this thesis attempts to answer.
